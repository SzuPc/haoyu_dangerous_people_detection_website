Abstract

We aim to detect and track dangerous individuals earlier in campus shooting scenarios.

In many publicly documented school shooting cases, the dangerous individual entered the monitored area before the attack and moved through hallways, entrances, or academic spaces while still appearing as an ordinary pedestrian. Detecting a weapon or a person in a single frame is not enough for response. The system must determine who is carrying the weapon and then maintain that identity over time under occlusion, turning, and cross-area movement. Motivated by this need, we propose a complete framework that combines person-weapon detection, dangerous person determination, and long-term tracking as a continuous visual perception task for campus shooting response.

This project is not about generic surveillance. It is driven by a more urgent question: when a campus shooting risk emerges, can a vision system identify the armed individual at the earliest possible stage and continue to know where that person is in every moment that follows?

For that reason, this page is not simply presenting another tracker. It follows the logic of real response: first explaining why overseas campus shooting scenarios require stronger dangerous person tracking, and then showing how our detection, association, and tracking pipeline turns weapon discovery into continuous localization of a dangerous individual.

What must be tracked is not only a bounding box, but a high-risk individual carrying a weapon and moving through a real campus environment.

Motivation

Campus shootings are not frequent, but every incident demands faster visual response.

The danger of campus shootings lies in their low-frequency but high-consequence nature. In many cases, the dangerous individual has already passed entrances, hallways, or building access points before the attack begins. The surveillance system may have already seen that person, but failed to form a continuous and actionable identity trajectory. For campus safety, the most important window often exists in the minutes before violence starts, not after it.

Public statistics from U.S. postsecondary institutions send a clear signal. Overall campus crime rates declined over the last decade, but extreme violence risk has not disappeared. Official education safety statistics report 18 active shooter incidents in U.S. postsecondary institutions from 2000 to 2022, causing 157 total casualties. In events of this kind, the ability to lock onto a dangerous person early can directly shape whether the surveillance system is useful for warning and response. This is also why we focus on tracking rather than stopping at single-frame detection. In a shooting risk scenario, the system must answer more than whether a gun appears in one frame. It must tell us where the armed individual entered, where that person is now, which areas have been crossed, and whether the same person reappears after temporary occlusion. Only by binding weapon cues to a continuous person trajectory can surveillance outputs become actionable in practice.

The key for response is not that a gun appears in one frame, but that the system continues to know where the armed person is.

Challenge

In shooting scenarios, tracking is both more important and more difficult than detection.

Dangerous person tracking cannot be solved by directly applying conventional MOT or ReID methods to campus footage. Shooting-risk scenes require the system to understand the relationship between people and weapons while preserving identity continuity through hallways, corners, occlusions, and fast motion. For real response, every identity switch, track loss, or false alarm can mean losing a critical intervention window.

People and weapons must be understood jointly

Shooting risk is not defined by the presence of a weapon alone, but by the spatial relationship between a weapon and a specific person. The system must determine which individual is carrying the weapon, rather than merely detecting an isolated weapon box in the image.

Targets move continuously through campus corridors

A dangerous individual may enter from a hallway access point and then move continuously through corridors, stairwells, or doorways. A single occlusion, turn, or temporary disappearance can break the trajectory, yet that continuous movement history is exactly what security personnel need most.

Real response requires continuous identity, not isolated snapshots

In campus shooting response, security systems must provide a continuously updated dangerous-person state rather than a handful of isolated detections. Video analysis only becomes practically useful when it can tell responders who the target is, where the target is now, and where that target has just been.

Method

Our method turns weapon discovery into continuous tracking of the armed individual.

As shown in the figure, we build a three-stage framework for campus shooting response. First, we train YOLOv12 on campus surveillance data to jointly detect people and weapon categories. Second, we determine the dangerous person through spatial overlap and distance rules between person boxes and weapon boxes. Third, we use SAM2 with memory attention to maintain long-term tracking of that dangerous individual, preserving identity continuity even under occlusion and pose variation.

Pipeline Overview

From left to right, the pipeline includes raw surveillance video input, a dataset covering people and weapons, YOLOv12 training and inference, rule-based dangerous person determination, and long-term tracking with SAM2. This design clearly separates what is detected from who actually constitutes the threat, and provides a clean target initialization for the downstream tracker.

Our framework first detects people and weapon instances from campus surveillance streams, then determines dangerous persons through spatial association rules, and finally maintains long-term identity-consistent tracking with SAM2.

Contributions

Our contributions

The contributions of this project lie in task formulation, system design, and interpretable validation, all centered on the practical demands of overseas campus shooting response.

1. A task formulation for campus shooting response

We formulate dangerous person detection and tracking as a continuous visual perception task for overseas campus shooting risk, emphasizing person-weapon association and long-term identity preservation as core requirements for practical response.

2. Unified detection, determination, and long-term tracking

We build a unified pipeline composed of YOLOv12 joint detection, rule-based dangerous person determination, and SAM2 long-term tracking, transforming weapon discovery into continuous localization of the armed individual.

3. Interpretable validation for real surveillance workflows

Through the architecture figure and video results, we directly visualize the full process from dangerous-person initialization to persistent tracking, making the system's stability, continuity, and usability observable in realistic campus shooting scenarios.
